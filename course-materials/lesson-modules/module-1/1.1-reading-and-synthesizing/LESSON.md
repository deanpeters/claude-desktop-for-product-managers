# Lesson 1.1: Reading and Synthesizing

## Objective

Use Claude to digest a folder of raw product context (company docs, discovery notes) and produce a one-page synthesis you'd actually stand behind in a meeting.

## Why and when

Synthesis is where AI pays off first for a product manager. You'll reach for this move whenever you inherit a product, come back from a run of customer interviews, get handed a folder of research the day before a kickoff, or need to brief an executive tomorrow morning. Turn raw context into a defensible one-pager quickly and every decision that follows starts from firmer ground. This is also the foundation move of the whole course: everything you build in later modules stands on your ability to trust what Claude read and catch what it guessed.

## Open these files

- `course-materials/company-context/`: the practice company overview and personas *(arrives in Phase 1; this lesson activates then)*
- `course-materials/exercises/discovery-notes/`: raw customer discovery notes

## Suggested prompts

```text
Read everything in company-context. Give me the shortest useful summary
of what this company does, who it serves, and what it's worried about.
```

```text
Now read the discovery notes. Synthesize: what problem keeps coming up,
who has it worst, and what did customers say verbatim that supports that?
Flag anything that's your inference rather than their words.
```

## Good result

- A synthesis grounded in the actual files, with quotes traceable to sources
- Inference clearly separated from evidence
- Short enough to read aloud in two minutes

## Review checklist

- Can you point to the file behind every claim?
- Did Claude flag its own inferences, or did you have to ask?
- Would you defend this summary to your VP without re-reading the sources?

## Extension

Ask Claude to argue the *opposite* read of the same notes: what's the strongest case that the recurring complaint is noise, not signal? Decide which read you believe and why.
