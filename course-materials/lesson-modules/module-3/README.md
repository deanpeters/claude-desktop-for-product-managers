# Module 3: Shaping the opportunity

The learn-fast core of the course. Low-res idea in, high-res decision out: from a well-framed problem to a tested concept and a real call.

## Lessons

- `3.1-framing-the-problem/` — a crisp, defensible problem statement
- `3.2-mapping-and-choosing/` — opportunity options and an explicit choice
- `3.3-positioning-the-idea/` — who it's for, what it replaces, why it wins
- `3.4-naming-the-bet/` — the riskiest assumption as a testable claim, plus the win condition: *"If we ___ for ___ then ___. We know we have succeeded when ___ and ___."* A solution hypothesis beats a hunch every time.
- `3.5-minimum-viable-narrative/` — six things, nothing more: user, situation and problem, riskiest assumption, win condition, interaction sequence, what's explicitly excluded. Anything else is scope creep wearing a narrative's clothes.
- `3.6-prototyping-with-artifacts/` — turn the narrative into something clickable. Brief like a collaborator: describe the goal, not the screens; hand over the narrative and guardrails, not a wireframe; correct the AI at least once.
- `3.7-pressure-test-and-decide/` — run the prototype against the win condition: pass, fail, or inconclusive. Then decide: go, no-go, or revise and retest. No maybe. The lesson is never "AI is stupid" — if the output wandered, the brief had a hole.

## Module rule

A prototype existing proves nothing. This module isn't done until the learner has made — and defended — a go/no-go/revise call on their own concept.
